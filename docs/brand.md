# Brand

## Positioning

For electronic music fans who actually plan their days and nights — from week-long festivals to a party that ends at 6am —

Earrands is the companion that replaces the screenshot habit, the WhatsApp chaos, and the fifteen browser tabs.

Built around how people actually move through an event: discovering artists, locking in sets, coordinating with friends, and remembering what they heard.

Works offline. Works in a group. Works when the signal doesn't.

## Brand Voice

### The one-sentence anchor

**Earrands talks like a knowledgeable friend at the festival, not like an app.**

That's the test for every word. Would a person who genuinely loves this music say this to another person who genuinely loves this music? If not, rewrite it.

The "true rave spirit vs. over-commercialization" is the philosophy behind the app.

---

### Four voice qualities

**1. Specific, not hype-y**

The culture you're building for has a finely tuned bullshit detector. Superlatives, exclamation marks, "amazing experience" language — all of it reads as festival main stage sponsor copy. Say the exact thing, nothing more.

```
✗  "Discover incredible artists and plan your perfect festival!"
✓  "Pick your sets. We'll handle the clashes."
```

**2. Warm but not try-hard**

The rave scene is genuinely welcoming — that should come through. But performed friendliness (excessive emoji, "hey you!" energy, startup-casual overdrive) reads as fake in a community that values authenticity.

```
✗  "Looks like you haven't added any sets yet 👀 Let's fix that!"
✓  "No sets added yet. Start with the stage you're most excited about."
```

**3. Functional first, human when it earns it**

Most of the app is UI. It doesn't need a personality — it needs to work. Reserve the voice for the moments where a human would actually say something: post-set rating, empty states after an event ends, onboarding, errors.

```
Timetable UI: silent. Labels, times, names. Nothing else.

Post-set rating: "How was it?" — three words, done.
Or after a thumbs down: "Noted. More room for the next one."
```

**4. Underground credibility without gatekeeping**

"If you know you know" doesn't mean excluding people. It means not explaining the culture to people who already live in it. Assume the user knows what a b2b set is. Assume they've heard of the artists. Don't write like you're onboarding someone who has never been to a festival.

```
✗  "Rate the sets you've seen to build your personal music history!"
✓  "Rate your sets. Build your history."
```

### Voice in specific moments

| Moment | Tone | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Onboarding | Minimal, direct | "Your festival. Your schedule. No account needed to start." |
| Empty timetable | Neutral, practical | "Festival added. Now pick your sets." |
| Set clash warning | Useful, no drama | "Conflicts with Blawan at Shelter. 10 min walk between stages." |
| Post-set rating | Human, brief | "How was it?" / "Worth the walk?" |
| After thumbs down | Dry, light | "Noted. More room for the next one." |
| Offline mode active | Reassuring, matter-of-fact | "You're offline. Everything still works." |
| Schedule shared | Flat, confident | "Link copied. Send it to the group." |
| End of festival | Warmer than usual | "That's a wrap. X sets, X hours. Good run." |
| Error state | Honest, no spin | "Something went wrong loading this lineup. Try again when you have signal." |

---

### What this voice never does

- Calls the user "music lover" or any demographic label
- Uses "seamless," "experience," "journey," "discover your sound"
- Adds emoji to functional messages
- Explains the culture back to the user
- Apologizes excessively for errors
- Ends sentences with exclamation marks unless something genuinely warrants excitement (rare)

---

### The one test

Before publishing any copy, read it out loud and ask: *would this feel out of place on a flyer for a night at Shelter or Treehouse?*

If yes, rewrite it.
